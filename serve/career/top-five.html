Top Five Strengths

1. Arranger
  • I am resourceful; I will find a way to get it to work.
  • I thrive in situations where I have many things going on at the same time.
  • I am flexible, in how I organize people and how I configure space.
  • I need to be in a dynamic area of an organization. Routine, static part of an organization drains my energy.
  • I am always looking for the perfect configuration.
Action!

Ideas For Action

  1. Be sure to keep track of ongoing deadlines for your many tasks, projects, and obligations. Although you enjoy the chance to juggle lots activities, others with less powerful Arranger talents may become anxious if they don't see you working on their projects frequently. Inform them of your progress to ease their fears.
  2. Seek complex, dynamic environments in which there are few routines.
  3. Take on the organization of a big event; a convention, a large party, or a company celebration.
  4. Give people time to understand your way of doing things when you present it to them. Your mental juggling is instinctive, but others might find it difficult to break with existing procedures. Take the time to clearly explain why your way can be more effective.
  5. Figure out how you can improve workflow by rearranging space and/or procedures to maximize efficiency and to free up time for you and for others.
2. Learner
  • I am eager to learn new facts, skills or knowledge.
  • I seek roles that requires some form of technical competence.
  • I prefer fields with constantly changing technologies or regulations.
  • I am drawn to the process of learning rather than the content or the result.
Action!

Ideas for Action

  1. Refine how you learn. For example, you might learn best by teaching; if so, seek out opportunities to present to others. You might learn best through quiet reflection; if so, find this quiet time.
  2. Develop ways to track the progress of your learning. If there are distinct levels or stages of learning within a discipline or skill, take a moment to celebrate your progression from one level to the next. If no such levels exist, create them for yourself ( e.g., reading five books on the subject or making three presentations on the subject).
  3. Be a catalyst for change. Others might be intimidated by new rules, new skills, or new circumstances. Your willingness to soak up this newness can calm their fears and spur them to action. Take this responsibility seriously.
  4. Honor your desire to learn. Take advantage of adult educational opportunities in your community. Discipline yourself to sign up for at least one new academic or adult learning courses each year.
  5. Time disappears and your attention intensifies when you are immersed in studying or learning. Allow yourself to "follow the trail" by scheduling learning sessions during periods of time that will not be interrupted by pressing engagements.
3. Adaptability
  • I live in the moment. I don't resent sudden requests.
  • I am a very flexible person that can stay productive when demands of work are pulling me in many directions at once.
  • I enjoy roles which success depends on responding to constantly changing circumstances.
  • I am most productive on short term assignments that requires immediate action.
  • I prefer life with quick skirmishes rather than long drawn out campaigns.
Action!

Ideas for Action

  1. Cultivate your reputation as calm and reassuring person when others become upset by daily events.
  2. Seeks roles in which success depends on responding to constantly changing circumstances. Consider career areas such as journalism, live television production, emergency healthcare, and customer service. In these roles, the best react the fastest and stay levelheaded.
  3. Look to others for planning. People who have strong Focus, Strategic, or Belief talents can help you shape your long-term goals, leaving you to excel at dealing with the day-to-day variations.
  4. Avoid tasks that are too structured and stifle your need for variety. If given a list of tasks to complete, try to indulge your desire for flexibility by making a game of that list. See if you can be creative or make the tasks more fun in some way.
  5. Openly use your reassuring demeanor to soothe disgruntled friends or coworkers. Think about the approach you used, and remember to apply it again when the situation presents itself.
4. Empathy
  • I am sensitive to the emotions of others / I can sense the emotions of those around me.
  • I can easily gauge the emotional tone of a room; I have the talent to forge a bridge of understanding and mutual support.
  • People are drawn to me because I help them give voice to their emotional life.
  • I hear the unvoiced questions, and I anticipate the needs of others.
Action!

Ideas For Action

  1. Partner with someone with strong Command or Activator talents. This person will help you take needed action, even though people's feeling might suffer as result.
  2. Consider serving others as a confidante or mentor. Because trust is paramount to you, people are likely to feel comfortable approaching you with any need. Your discretion and desire to be genuinely helpful be grealty valued.
  3. Witnessing the happiness of others brings your pleasure. Consequently, you are likely to be attuned to opportunities to underscore other's successes and positively reinforce their achievements. At each opportunity, deliver a kind word of appreciation or recognition. In doing so, you are likely to make a profound and engaging impression.
  4. Because you are observant of how others are feeling, you are likely to intuit what is about to happen before it becomes common knowledge. Although your intuitions may at times seem nothing more than "hunches", take conscious note of them. They may turn out to be valuable assets.
  5. Sometimes empathy does not require words at all. A kind gesture may be all someone needs to be reassured. Use your Empathy talents to nonverbally comfort others with a glance, a smile, or a pat on the arm.
5. Individualization
  • I am intrigued by the unique qualities of each person, I focus on the differences between individuals.
  • Because of my keen observation of other's strengths, I can draw out the best in each person, which helps me build productive teams.
  • I move comfortably around broad range of style and cultures, and intuitively personalize my interaction.
  • I am easily able to understand someone else's perspective.
Action!

Ideas For Action

  1. Select a vocatoin in which your Individualization talents can be both used and appreciated, such as counselling, supervising, teaching, or selling. Your ability to see people as unique individuals is a special talent.
  2. Become an expert in describing your own strengths and style. For example, answer questions such as: What is the best praise you ever received? How often do you like to check in with your manager? What is the best method for building relationships? How do you learn best? Then ask your colleagues and friends these same questions. Help them plan their future by starting with their strengths, then designing a future based on what they do best.
  3. Figure out what every person on your team does best. Then help them capitalize on their talents, skills, and knowledge. You may need to explain your rationale and your philosophy so people understand that you have their best interests in mind.
  4. Your presentation and speaking opportunities will be most engaging when you relate your topic to the experiences of individuals in the audience. Use your Individualization talents to gather and share real-life stories that will make your points much better than would generic information or theories.
  5. You move comfortably among a broad range of styles and cultures, and you intuitively personalize your interactions. Consciously and proactively make full use of these talents by leading diversity and community efforts.
